ESS 330 Final Project Guidelines

Scientists use the scientific method to answer their research questions. These questions are based on detailed observations and on a review of the available scientific literature to identify knowledge gaps where further research is required. A good research project needs a specific question or objective, testable hypotheses, and clear predictions. After these first steps, scientists collect the data, conduct appropriate analyses, and report the results. Presentation of the results can be in several formats, such as a formal scientific paper, conference presentation, formal report, web application, outreach activity, etc. However, properly reporting the results is a key step to shareing your findings with the scientific community and beyond.

Often researchers must present a “blueprint” or proposal of the upcoming study to solicit funding, get the proper permissions from environmental and academic authorities, or to keep record of the proposed study. This proposal usually includes an introduction of the topic and identifies its significance, presents the proposed methodology to be used to collect and analyze the data, and other sections such as a schedule and budget (not relevant for this class). For proposals, the methods section is written in future tense and not past tense, because it describes a project that has not been done yet. Writing a research proposal is a key step because it helps to clarify the scope and predictions of the study, identifies important variables and reduces chances of over manipulation of the data later to make it fit the expected outcome.

In groups of 2-3 you will outline and develop your own project to answer a particular research question of interest. Your research question(s) can cover any area related to ecosystem science and sustainability, and you will use the coding and statistical skills you’ve learned in class so far to analyze and report your results. The project must have a clear question/hypothesis that you can answer given the datasets available and time frame of completing the project. You will start by developing proposals for two distinct projects/research questions, then based on feedback from your TA and your personal preference, you will choose one of your proposed projects to move forward with as your final project. Your final project will have both an oral (lightning talks) and written (formal R Markdown report) component and must use at least two analyses we’ve learned in class to answer your research question(s).

Final Projects will have 3 milestones:

  1. Project proposal (Lab 5)
  2. Lightning talk presentations (Final Lab Section)
  3. Full project report (Due before your lightning talks)

Work in groups of 2-3 (within your lab sections)

Getting Started:

Choose a research question

The Canvas Discussion Board where you brainstormed potential research topics/questions earlier in the semester is pinned in Canvas. You can pull ideas from here or develop entirely new ideas!

Find datasets

In class we will conduct a ‘data scavenger hunt’, compiling as many open-source datasets as possible, categorized by topic and type of data. The results of this scavenger hunt will be shared as an excel spreadsheet you can explore in lab while developing your project proposals.

Project Proposal:

Done as lab 5, your project proposals will get you thinking about possible topics to study along with there interts, feasibility, and scope. Your TA’s will work with you to select one of your proposals as the basis for your final projects.

Lightning Talks and Final Report:

Your final projects have two components, an oral presentation in the form of a lightning talk and a written presentation in the form of a formal report produced using Quarto.

Lightning talks:

A lightning talk is a brief (~5-10 minute) presentation that encompasses the essence of your research project. Being so short, they require you to condense all your work into the most important highlights of your study and results. Your talk should include the background of your study, research questions/hypotheses, your results (at least 2 visuals), and end with the main conclusions and what you think is most important point for your audience to take away from your study.

These presentations will take place during your final lab section. Since you are working in groups, you can choose to either present as a group, or designate a single presenter (but allocate the rest of the work accordingly). This article has some tips on lightning talks.

Formal Report:

Your final, formal report should follow the organization of a scientific paper but will be written in Quarto and submitted as a deployed Github Link. It can be an expansion of your project proposal, but now must be written in past tense since you completed the project. It must include the following sections:

  1. Title:

  2. Abstract Your title should adequately and concisely describe your project and findings. Title should be followed by author names and institution. The abstract should briefly summarize each section of the paper (normally ~ 1 sentence for each section).

  3. Introduction/Hypothesis Your introduction should start by describing a problem or question. It should then present sufficient background information and describe the importance of the study to the scientific community and any knowledge gaps you are addressing. You should compare your work to previous research. This is the section that will have the most citations. The introduction should end with the objectives and hypotheses being tested.

  4. Methods Methods sections should provide sufficient detail to allow someone to replicate your work. Describe the scope of your study, the study system (e.g., species, ecosystem, geographic location, etc.), datasets used (with citations), the process of compiling, formatting, and/or transforming the data, and the analyses used to test your hypotheses.

  5. Results Your results section must include the statistical results of at least two different analyses (reported in proper scientific format) along with a minimum of two figures. In addition to two figures, you may also include tables. Unlike a normal scientific paper, in the results section you should also include the code you ran to conduct the analyses. This includes any code used to test for assumptions, plus the final analysis (similar to how you completed homework assignments in Quarto).

  6. Discussion/Conclusions In the discussion you should interpret and critically evaluate all your results. Compare your findings to other published related research and report final conclusions in relation to your original objectives and hypotheses.

  7. Citations/References You should have a minimum of 10 peer-reviewed primary literature sources (scientific journal articles). You can include data citations in this total of 10 sources. Every article/resource in the References section must be cited somewhere in your formal report.
